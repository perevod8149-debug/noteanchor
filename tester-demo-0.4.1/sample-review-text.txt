The committee met in the small upstairs room just after noon, when the light was already too pale for comfort and yet too bright for anyone to ignore the dust on the window ledge. Several people said the meeting had been productive, although no one seemed eager to explain what had actually been settled.

At one point the report described the plan as both immediate and gradual, which made it sound careful at first and confused a moment later. A reader could follow the sentence, but only with the uneasy feeling that two opposite promises had been tied together and presented as a single thought.

Marian read the second page twice because one phrase kept troubling her: the town would be renewed through modest boldness. She circled the words with her pencil and waited for them to become clearer, but they remained awkward in exactly the same way.

Near the bottom of the page, another paragraph praised the market square for its timeless new character. The description was not false, exactly, yet it seemed too smooth and too proud, as if the writer wanted admiration before giving evidence.

When the meeting ended, everyone carried away a different idea of what should happen next. One man wanted a public statement. Another preferred a private letter. A third thought the whole matter should rest for a week before anyone spoke at all.

By evening the notes on the table had become more useful than the official summary. A few short remarks, placed beside exact phrases, told the truth more plainly than the polished draft: unclear, too vague, define this, check source, stronger example needed.
