When the rain stopped, the street did not brighten at once. The stones kept their dark color, and the puddles held a weak copy of the sky. A woman crossed the square with a basket on her arm and walked as if she had somewhere exact to be, though the whole town seemed to move more slowly after the storm.

In the upper window of the inn, a lamp had been left burning through the afternoon. Its light was hardly needed, yet it gave the room a patient, inhabited look. A traveler standing below might have thought that someone inside was reading a letter, mending a sleeve, or waiting for a knock that had not yet come.

At the corner, two boys argued about the river road. One said it was shorter, the other said it was safer, and both spoke with the confidence of people who had never had to arrive before nightfall. An older man listened without interrupting, then smiled in the tired way of someone who has walked both roads often enough to distrust easy certainty.

The baker's door stood open, and the smell of warm bread drifted into the damp air. It did not transform the street, but it changed its mood. For a moment the whole place felt less abandoned by the weather and more returned to itself, as if ordinary work had quietly resumed its rights.

By dusk the clouds had broken into long pale strips over the fields beyond the last houses. Nothing remarkable happened then. A cart rolled past, a gate shut somewhere out of sight, and the first evening star appeared above the roofs. Still, the hour carried that small and difficult feeling that the day had not ended in disappointment, only in silence.
